## Quick Summary
Absolute advantage means producing a good using fewer resources than another party, while comparative advantage means producing it at a lower opportunity cost, that is, giving up less of other goods. The key insight is that gains from trade depend on comparative, not absolute, advantage. Even if one country is better at making everything, both parties still benefit if each specializes in what it sacrifices least to produce and then trades.

Why do countries trade at all, and why can both sides come out ahead? The answer is one of the most surprising and powerful ideas in economics, and it rests on a careful distinction. **Absolute advantage** means being able to produce a good using fewer resources, such as fewer labor hours, than someone else. **Comparative advantage**, by contrast, means being able to produce a good at a lower **opportunity cost**, that is, by giving up less of other things. The deep insight, first set out by David Ricardo, is that mutually beneficial trade depends on comparative advantage, not absolute advantage. This is really an application of [[Opportunity Cost and Trade-offs]] across borders.

Consider a simple numerical example with two countries, Portugal and England, each able to produce wine and cloth. Suppose in one day a Portuguese worker can make either **6 units of wine** or **3 units of cloth**, while an English worker can make either **2 units of wine** or **2 units of cloth**. Portugal is better at both, so it has the absolute advantage in everything. Yet look at opportunity costs. For Portugal, making 6 wine means giving up 3 cloth, so each unit of wine costs **half a unit of cloth**. For England, making 2 wine means giving up 2 cloth, so each wine costs **a full unit of cloth**. Portugal gives up less cloth to make wine, so its comparative advantage is in wine; England, by the same logic, has the comparative advantage in cloth.

Now let each country **specialize** in what it sacrifices least to produce. Portugal pours its effort into wine, England into cloth, and then they trade. Because each is doing what it gives up the least to do, the combined output of wine and cloth is larger than if each tried to make both. Trade then lets each country consume a bundle of wine and cloth that lies **beyond** what it could have produced alone. Both parties end up better off, even though one was more productive at everything. That is the magic: trade is not a contest one side wins, but a way of enlarging the total pie through specialization.

These **gains from trade** are why economists are broadly in favor of open international trade, and why consumers around the world enjoy cheaper and more varied goods, from coffee to smartphones, than any single country could produce efficiently on its own. Specialization also lets producers achieve larger scale and sharper expertise.

But the picture deserves balance. Trade creates **winners and losers within** each country, not just gains for the nation as a whole. When a country imports cheap cloth, its domestic cloth makers may shrink and lay off workers, even as exporters, importers, and shoppers gain. Governments sometimes respond with **tariffs**, taxes on imports meant to protect home industries. Tariffs can shield specific jobs, but they also raise prices for everyone and shrink the overall gains from trade, often protecting a few jobs at a high cost to many consumers. The honest conclusion is that trade tends to make countries richer in total, but spreading those gains fairly, and helping the people and towns hurt by foreign competition, is a real and important challenge.
