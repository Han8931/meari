## Quick Summary
Supply describes how much of a good producers are willing and able to sell at each price, and the law of supply states that, holding other factors constant, a higher price increases quantity supplied while a lower price decreases it. This positive relationship gives an upward-sloping supply curve, because higher prices make production more profitable and cover the rising marginal cost of producing extra units.

If demand is the buyers' side of a market, **supply** is the sellers' side. Supply tells us how much of a good producers are *willing and able* to bring to market at each possible price. Just like demand, it's a whole schedule rather than a single figure, because the amount firms want to sell depends heavily on the price they can get.

The guiding principle here is the **law of supply**: all else being equal, the higher the price of a good, the *more* of it producers want to sell, and the lower the price, the less. This is a *positive* relationship, the opposite of demand, which is why the **supply curve** slopes upward. Picture a bakery: at $2 a loaf it might bake 200 loaves a day, but at $4 a loaf the extra profit makes it worth firing up a second oven and hiring help to produce 350. Higher prices reward sellers for expanding output.

There's a deeper reason for the upward slope: producing additional units usually costs more at the margin. The first loaves use the cheapest, easiest resources, but squeezing out extra production means paying overtime, buying pricier ingredients, or running equipment harder. Producers will only take on those rising costs if the price is high enough to make it worthwhile.

As with demand, beginners must separate a **movement along** the supply curve from a **shift** of the whole curve. When only the good's *own price* changes, we slide along the existing curve, this is a change in *quantity supplied*. The bakery example above is a movement along the curve. The entire curve shifts only when something *other* than the good's price changes, which we call a change in *supply*.

The main **determinants of supply** that shift the curve are:

- **Input prices** — cheaper flour, fuel, or wages lowers production costs and shifts supply right; costlier inputs shift it left.
- **Technology** — a faster oven or automated packing line lets firms produce more at every price, shifting supply right.
- **Expectations** — if sellers expect higher prices next month, they may hold back inventory now, shifting current supply left.
- **Number of sellers** — when new bakeries open, market supply expands.

Getting this distinction right changes your analysis. A sudden jump in wheat prices doesn't cause a movement along the curve, it shifts the whole supply curve leftward, so less is offered at *every* price. Supply, however, only determines outcomes when matched against [[Demand]]; together they settle the actual price and quantity at [[Market Equilibrium]].
