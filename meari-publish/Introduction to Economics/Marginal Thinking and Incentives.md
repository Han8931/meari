## Quick Summary
Marginal thinking means evaluating decisions in small, incremental steps rather than all-or-nothing. A rational agent should take an action as long as its *marginal benefit*—the extra benefit from one more unit—exceeds its *marginal cost*, the extra cost of that unit, and stop where the two are equal. A factory deciding whether to run an extra shift compares the revenue from the additional output with the additional wages and energy, ignoring fixed costs already incurred.

Most important decisions in life are not "all or nothing"—they are "how much" or "one more?" Should you study for one *more* hour? Should a bakery bake one *more* tray of croissants? Should a city add one *more* bus to a route? Economists answer these with **marginal thinking**: evaluating the costs and benefits of *one additional unit* of something rather than the total. The word *marginal* simply means "additional" or "at the edge."

The core rule is elegant. For each extra unit, compare the **marginal benefit** (the additional satisfaction or revenue you gain) with the **marginal cost** (the additional cost you incur). A rational agent keeps doing the activity as long as marginal benefit exceeds marginal cost, and stops at the point where the two are equal. Consider a coffee stand deciding how long to stay open. The 7 a.m. hour might bring in $90 of sales at a cost of $20—clearly worth it. By 9 p.m. an extra hour might bring only $15 while wages and electricity cost $25; that hour is a loss. The optimal closing time is where the last profitable hour ends. Notice that this reasoning deliberately ignores money already spent on the espresso machine—those are sunk costs, as discussed in [[Opportunity Cost and Trade-offs]]. Marginal thinking also underlies how firms set output to maximise profit, a theme developed in [[Costs Production and Profit]].

Why do people change how much they do? Because of **incentives**—rewards that make an action more attractive or penalties that make it less so. An incentive works by shifting the marginal benefit or marginal cost of a choice:

- A **subsidy** on solar panels lowers their cost, so households install more.
- A **tax** on cigarettes raises their price, so some smokers cut back.
- A **bonus** for hitting a sales target raises the marginal benefit of one more sale.

This is one of the most powerful ideas in economics: **rational people respond to incentives**. Prices themselves are incentives—when something becomes scarce its price rises (see [[Demand]] and [[Supply]]), encouraging buyers to economize and sellers to produce more. Almost every policy lever, from carbon taxes to traffic fines, ultimately works by changing the incentives people face.

But incentives are powerful precisely because people respond to them—including in ways the designer never intended. History is full of incentives gone wrong:

- **The cobra effect.** Colonial officials in Delhi offered a bounty for every dead cobra to reduce the snake population. Enterprising residents began *breeding* cobras to collect the reward; when the scheme was scrapped, the worthless snakes were released, leaving more cobras than before.
- **The daycare fine.** When Israeli daycares fined parents for picking up children late, *more* parents came late. The fine reframed lateness from a moral lapse into a small fee that could simply be paid.
- **Teaching to the test.** Paying or ranking teachers strictly by exam scores can lead to drilling and even cheating rather than genuine learning.

The lesson is not that incentives are bad, but that they must be **aligned** with the outcome you actually want. Smart institutions—and smart governments designing taxes, subsidies, and regulations (see [[Fiscal Policy and Government Budgets]])—ask a careful question before acting: "Given that people will respond rationally at the margin, what behaviour will this incentive *really* produce?" Anticipating that response is the difference between a policy that works and one that backfires.
