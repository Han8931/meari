## Quick Summary
If CPI inflation is 5% while wages rise 3%, real wages fall by about 2%, because purchasing power depends on wages after adjusting for prices. Inflation is a sustained rise in the general price level, commonly tracked by the Consumer Price Index (CPI), a basket of goods and services households buy. It can be caused by demand-pull pressure, when spending outruns supply, or cost-push pressure, when input costs such as oil or wages rise.

Two numbers dominate the headlines whenever people discuss the health of an economy: how fast prices are rising and how many people are out of work. **Inflation** and **unemployment** are the twin barometers of macroeconomics, and understanding them, along with the tension between them, is essential to making sense of economic news and policy.

**Inflation** is a sustained increase in the *general* price level, not just a jump in the price of one product. The most familiar gauge is the **Consumer Price Index (CPI)**, which tracks the cost of a fixed "basket" of goods and services that a typical household buys, things like food, rent, gasoline, and clothing. If that basket cost $1,000 last year and $1,030 this year, the CPI implies an inflation rate of about 3%. Inflation generally springs from two sources. **Demand-pull** inflation occurs when total spending outruns the economy's capacity to produce, too much money chasing too few goods. **Cost-push** inflation occurs when the cost of inputs like oil or wages rises, pushing producers to raise prices. Because inflation is fundamentally about the price level, it is closely tied to how we measure output (see [[Measuring the Economy with GDP]]).

Why does inflation matter? Most importantly, it **erodes purchasing power**: if prices rise 4% but your wage stays flat, you can afford 4% less than before, so your real income has fallen. It eats away at the value of **savings** held in cash and at fixed-rate assets. It can **redistribute wealth arbitrarily**, helping borrowers, who repay loans in cheaper dollars, at the expense of lenders, who are repaid in money worth less than they lent. And when inflation becomes high or unpredictable, it distorts decision-making, blurs the price signals that markets rely on, and forces households and firms to waste effort coping with constantly changing prices. A little, steady inflation is generally considered manageable; rapid or volatile inflation is genuinely damaging.

The second great barometer is **unemployment**, the percentage of people in the labor force, those who are working or actively seeking work, who do not have a job. Economists distinguish three main types:

- **Frictional unemployment** is short-term and largely healthy, the normal time it takes people to search for and transition between jobs, such as a graduate looking for their first position or someone who quit to find a better fit.
- **Structural unemployment** reflects a deeper mismatch between the skills, technology, or location of available workers and the jobs on offer, for example, factory workers displaced by automation whose old skills no longer match new openings.
- **Cyclical unemployment** rises and falls with the business cycle, climbing during recessions when weak demand leads firms to lay workers off and shrinking during expansions.

Some unemployment always exists even in a strong economy, because frictional and structural joblessness never fully disappear; this baseline is called the **natural rate of unemployment**.

Finally, inflation and unemployment are linked by a famous **short-run trade-off**. When an economy runs hot, with brisk spending and low unemployment, businesses compete for scarce workers and materials, bidding up wages and prices, so inflation tends to rise. When the economy cools and unemployment climbs, that price pressure eases. This inverse relationship is the core idea behind the **Phillips curve**, which historically suggested policymakers could "buy" lower unemployment at the cost of higher inflation, or vice versa. The catch is that this trade-off is reliable only in the short run. Over the long run, once people come to expect a given rate of inflation and build it into wages and prices, the relationship breaks down, and an economy can suffer high inflation and high unemployment at the same time, a painful combination known as **stagflation**.
