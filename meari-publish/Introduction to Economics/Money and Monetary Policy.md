## Quick Summary
Money is anything widely accepted in exchange, and it performs three jobs: a medium of exchange that removes the need for barter's double coincidence of wants, a store of value that holds purchasing power over time, and a unit of account that lets us price everything in common terms like dollars. Most modern money is bank deposits created through lending, while the central bank influences the supply of money and credit mainly by steering short-term interest rates and banking conditions.

Imagine a world without money. A baker who wants shoes would have to find a shoemaker who happens to want bread, at the same moment, in the right amounts. Economists call this the problem of the **double coincidence of wants**, and it makes barter painfully slow. Money solves it. **Money** is anything widely accepted as payment, and it does three jobs at once. As a **medium of exchange**, it lets the baker sell bread to anyone and use the proceeds to buy shoes. As a **store of value**, it lets you hold the proceeds of today's sale and spend them next month. As a **unit of account**, it gives us a common yardstick, so a loaf might cost $3 and a pair of shoes $60, and we can compare them instantly.

Modern money is mostly not gold or even paper. The bulk of it is numbers in bank accounts, created when banks make loans. Sitting at the center of this system is a **central bank**, like the U.S. Federal Reserve or the European Central Bank. The central bank does not run ordinary shops or lend to you directly; instead it influences the overall supply of money and credit, acts as a banker to the commercial banks, and steers the economy by influencing **interest rates**, the price of borrowing money.

Here is the key lever. The central bank sets a target for a short-term interest rate, and that rate ripples out into mortgage rates, business loans, and credit cards. When the central bank **lowers** rates, borrowing becomes cheap. A family is more likely to take out a mortgage; a firm is more likely to borrow to build a new factory. Spending and investment rise, which boosts demand, hiring, and output. Economists call this **expansionary** or "loose" policy.

When the central bank **raises** rates, the opposite happens. Borrowing costs more, so a $300,000 mortgage that cost $1,500 a month at a low rate might cost $1,900 at a higher one. Households postpone big purchases, firms shelve expansion plans, and total spending cools. This **contractionary** or "tight" policy slows the economy down on purpose.

Why slow it down deliberately? Mainly to control **inflation**, the general rise in prices over time. If demand races ahead of what the economy can produce, prices climb. By raising rates and damping spending, the central bank brings demand back in line and eases inflation. For example, after inflation spiked above 8% in 2022, the Federal Reserve raised its key rate from near zero to over 5% to cool things off. The catch is the trade-off: the same higher rates that tame inflation also slow growth and can push up unemployment. This tension between stable prices and full employment is the heart of central banking, and it connects directly to [[Inflation and Unemployment]].
