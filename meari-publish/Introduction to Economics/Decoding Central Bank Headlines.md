## Quick Summary
When a headline says the Fed "raised rates by 25 basis points," it means the central bank lifted its benchmark short-term interest rate by a quarter of a percentage point to fight inflation by cooling spending and borrowing. This ripples outward: variable mortgages, credit cards, and business loans get more expensive, savings accounts pay a bit more, and hiring may slow.

Few news items move the world economy like a central bank meeting. When you read "**The Fed** raised rates by **25 basis points**," the jargon is hiding a simple idea. A central bank — the **Federal Reserve** in the US, the **ECB** in the eurozone, the Bank of England in the UK — sets a benchmark short-term interest rate that anchors the cost of borrowing across the whole economy. A **basis point** is just one-hundredth of a percentage point, so 25 basis points is a quarter of a percent and 100 basis points is a full percent. Headlines use basis points because central banks move in small, deliberate steps and the precision matters.

Why does the bank move rates at all? It is the main lever of [[Money and Monetary Policy]]. When inflation runs hot, the bank **raises** rates to make borrowing pricier, which cools spending and investment and eventually slows price increases. When the economy is weak and jobs are disappearing, it **cuts** rates to make money cheap and encourage activity. This is the core trade-off behind [[Inflation and Unemployment]]: squeeze too hard and you risk a recession, go too easy and inflation lingers.

The words around the number often matter more than the number itself. Watch for these:

- **Hawkish** — leaning toward higher rates to fight inflation, even at the cost of growth. Hawks worry about prices.
- **Dovish** — leaning toward lower rates to support jobs and growth. Doves worry about unemployment.
- **Guidance / the dot plot** — the bank's hints about where rates are headed next. Markets trade on expectations, so a hint of "two more hikes this year" can shake stocks more than the actual decision.
- **Quantitative easing (QE)** — the bank creating money to buy bonds, pushing long-term rates down and pumping liquidity into the system (used in crises).
- **Quantitative tightening (QT)** — the reverse: the bank shrinks its bond holdings, draining money from the system.

This is why a "rate hold" can still crash markets. If a decision was fully expected, it is already "priced in," and the real news is the *tone*. A headline like "Fed holds, but signals rates higher for longer" is hawkish guidance, and investors sell because future borrowing looks costlier and growth slower. The lesson: read the press-conference language, not just the headline number.

Finally, trace how a rate decision reaches your life. A higher benchmark rate feeds into:

- **Mortgages and loans** — variable-rate borrowers pay more each month; new fixed mortgages get pricier.
- **Savings** — banks slowly raise the interest paid on deposits, so savers gain.
- **Jobs and wages** — costlier credit can cool hiring and slow the economy, which is often the *point* when fighting inflation.

So when a central banker speaks, ask three questions: which way did rates move and by how many basis points, what tone (hawkish or dovish) did they strike about the future, and what problem — runaway prices or a weakening job market — are they actually trying to solve?
