## Quick Summary
Costs split into fixed costs, which do not change with output (like a bakery's monthly rent of $3,000), and variable costs, which rise with production (flour, labor). Total cost is their sum; average cost is total cost divided by units; marginal cost is the extra cost of one more unit. Profit also comes in two flavors.

Every firm that produces something faces costs, and understanding those costs is the key to understanding how businesses decide what and how much to make. Economists begin by splitting costs into two families. **Fixed costs** stay the same no matter how much you produce. Imagine a small bakery that pays $3,000 a month in rent and $1,000 for its ovens whether it bakes one loaf or ten thousand. **Variable costs**, by contrast, rise and fall with output: flour, sugar, electricity for the ovens, and the wages of the bakers. Add the two together and you get **total cost**.

From total cost we derive two more useful numbers. **Average cost** is simply total cost divided by the number of units produced; if the bakery spends $6,000 to make 2,000 loaves, its average cost is $3 per loaf. **Marginal cost** is more subtle but more important: it is the additional cost of producing just one more unit. If making the 2,001st loaf adds $1.50 in flour, labor, and power, then $1.50 is the marginal cost of that loaf. Notice the rent does not enter the marginal calculation at all, because it does not change when one more loaf is baked.

Profit seems like a simple idea, but economists measure it in two different ways, and the gap between them matters enormously. **Accounting profit** is what most people mean by profit: revenue minus the explicit, out-of-pocket costs a firm actually writes checks for. **Economic profit** goes further and also subtracts **implicit costs**, the value of opportunities given up. Suppose the baker quit a $60,000-a-year job to open the shop. That forgone salary is a real cost of running the bakery even though no check is ever written for it. So a bakery showing $40,000 in accounting profit might actually be running a $20,000 economic loss once the lost salary is counted.

This distinction connects directly to **opportunity cost**, the idea that the true cost of any choice is the next best alternative you sacrifice. Economic profit asks a sharper question than accounting profit: are the owner's money, time, and effort earning more here than they could earn in their best alternative use? When economic profit is positive, staying in business is genuinely worthwhile; when it is zero, the firm is doing exactly as well as its next best option and has no reason to switch.

So how much should a firm produce? The answer flows from **marginal thinking**, weighing the extra benefit of one more action against its extra cost (see [[Marginal Thinking and Incentives]]). A firm should keep expanding output as long as each additional unit brings in at least as much **marginal revenue** as it adds in **marginal cost**. The profit-maximizing quantity sits exactly where **marginal revenue equals marginal cost (MR = MC)**. Below that quantity, each new unit earns more than it costs, so producing more raises profit. Beyond it, each new unit costs more than it earns, dragging profit down.

A quick example makes the rule concrete. Suppose our baker can sell each extra loaf for $2.50. As long as the marginal cost of a loaf is below $2.50, baking it adds to profit. Once rising ingredient and labor costs push the marginal cost of the next loaf above $2.50, that loaf loses money and should not be made. The firm settles at the output where the cost of the last loaf just equals the $2.50 it fetches. This simple MR = MC logic underlies the production decisions of every firm, from a corner bakery to a global manufacturer.
