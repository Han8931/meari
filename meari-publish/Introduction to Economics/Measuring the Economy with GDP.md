## Quick Summary
A headline saying nominal GDP rose 6% but real GDP rose 2% means roughly four percentage points came from higher prices rather than more goods and services. GDP is the total market value of final goods and services produced within a country, and the expenditure approach breaks it into consumption, investment, government spending, and net exports: GDP = C + I + G + NX.

To talk sensibly about whether an economy is growing, shrinking, or stalling, we need a single number that captures its overall size. That number is **Gross Domestic Product**, or **GDP**: the total market value of all **final goods and services** produced within a country's borders during a given period, typically a year. Three words in that definition do a lot of work. *Final* means we count the finished bread but not the flour sold to the bakery, so we avoid double-counting intermediate goods. *Within a country's borders* means a Japanese-owned car factory in Ohio counts toward U.S. GDP. And *market value* means everything is added up in dollars, letting us combine cars, haircuts, and software into one figure.

The most intuitive way to compute GDP is the **expenditure approach**, which simply adds up everyone's spending on that final output. It has four components, captured by the formula **GDP = C + I + G + NX**. **Consumption (C)** is household spending on goods and services, from groceries to streaming subscriptions, and is usually the largest piece, often around two-thirds of GDP in a country like the United States. **Investment (I)** is business spending on new equipment, factories, and housing, plus additions to inventory, not stocks and bonds, which merely shuffle ownership. **Government spending (G)** covers public salaries, roads, and defense, but excludes transfer payments like Social Security, since those do not buy new production. **Net exports (NX)** is exports minus imports, added so that we count goods made domestically and subtract spending that flowed abroad.

A crucial complication is that prices change over time, which can make GDP misleading. **Nominal GDP** values output at current prices, so it can rise even if a country produces exactly the same goods, simply because those goods got more expensive. **Real GDP** strips out this effect by valuing output at the prices of a fixed **base year**, isolating the change in the actual quantity of goods and services. If nominal GDP grows 5% but prices rose 3%, real GDP, the figure economists care about, grew only about 2%. Because it reflects genuine production rather than inflation, real GDP is the standard yardstick for economic growth and, very roughly, for changes in living standards. This connection to the price level links GDP directly to the study of [[Inflation and Unemployment]].

For all its usefulness, GDP is a deliberately narrow measure and **misses a great deal**. It ignores unpaid but valuable work, the parent raising children at home, the neighbor volunteering at a shelter, because no money changes hands. It overlooks the **underground economy** of cash and illegal transactions. It says nothing about how income is distributed, so a country can post a large GDP while most people struggle. It counts cleaning up an oil spill as a positive while ignoring the environmental damage that made the cleanup necessary, and it places no value on leisure, health, or the quality of goods. A higher GDP is not automatically a happier or fairer society.

- **Counts:** market production of final goods and services
- **Misses:** unpaid household and volunteer work, the underground economy, environmental harm, leisure, income inequality, and overall well-being

Finally, GDP does not climb in a smooth, straight line. Economies move through the **business cycle**, recurring swings of **expansion**, when output and employment rise, and **recession**, when they fall. A common rule of thumb defines a recession as two consecutive quarters of falling real GDP, though official bodies weigh several indicators. These ups and downs play out around a long-run upward growth **trend**: even an economy that doubles in size over a few decades will experience booms and busts along the way. Tracking real GDP over time lets economists and policymakers see where in that cycle an economy currently sits and respond accordingly.
