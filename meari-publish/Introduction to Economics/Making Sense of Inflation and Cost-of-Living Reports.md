## Quick Summary
A headline like "inflation cooled to 3.1%" means the Consumer Price Index rose 3.1% over the past twelve months — slower than before, but prices are still rising, just less quickly. This is **disinflation**, not **deflation** (which is actual falling prices). The confusion many readers feel is real: a lower inflation rate does not undo earlier increases, so the cost of living stays elevated even as the rate falls.

Every month a statistics agency releases an inflation report, and the headline usually distills it to one number: "**Inflation cooled to 3.1%**." That figure almost always comes from the **Consumer Price Index (CPI)**, which tracks the price of a representative "basket" of goods and services a typical household buys — groceries, rent, fuel, transport, haircuts. The 3.1% is a **year-over-year** change: prices today are 3.1% higher than twelve months ago. Understanding that one phrase already separates you from most readers.

The single most common misunderstanding is this: **falling inflation does not mean falling prices.** When inflation drops from 6% to 3.1%, prices are still climbing — just more slowly. This slowdown is called **disinflation**. Genuinely falling prices, where inflation goes *below zero*, is **deflation**, and it is rare and often a warning sign of a collapsing economy. So when a relieved headline says "inflation eases" while shoppers grumble that groceries cost more than last year, both are right: the *rate* of increase slowed, but earlier increases are baked in for good. Prices rarely roll back.

A sharp reader also splits the number into two flavors:

- **Headline inflation** — the all-in figure, including volatile **food and energy** prices. It is what people feel at the pump and the supermarket.
- **Core inflation** — the same basket *minus* food and energy. Because oil and crop prices swing wildly month to month, stripping them out reveals the steadier underlying trend. Central banks lean on core to decide policy, which is why "headline cooled but core stayed sticky at 3.8%" can be bad news even when the big number looks friendly.

The figure that actually determines whether your life is getting better is the gap between prices and pay — the difference between **nominal** and **real**. Your **nominal wage** is the number on your payslip; your **real wage** adjusts it for inflation. A rough rule:

- Real wage change ≈ nominal wage growth − inflation.
- If pay rose **4%** and inflation is **3.1%**, you gained about **0.9%** in real purchasing power.
- If pay rose only **2%** against the same 3.1% inflation, you are roughly **1.1% poorer** despite a bigger paycheck — the "**cost-of-living squeeze**" headlines describe.

When the next report lands, run a quick mental checklist: Is it headline or core? Is the rate faster or slower than economists *expected* (markets care about surprises)? And how does it stack up against wage growth? These same numbers drive the central bank's rate decisions and feed directly into the broader story of [[Inflation and Unemployment]] — high inflation pushes policymakers to raise rates, which they hope will cool prices without throwing people out of work.
