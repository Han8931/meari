## Quick Summary
Market equilibrium is the price and quantity at which the quantity demanded exactly equals the quantity supplied, so there is no pressure for price to change. It sits where the demand and supply curves intersect. If the price is above equilibrium, quantity supplied exceeds quantity demanded, creating a surplus; sellers cut prices to clear unsold stock, pushing price down toward equilibrium.

Neither buyers nor sellers set prices on their own. Prices emerge from the interaction between [[Demand]] and [[Supply]], and the special point where the two forces balance is called **market equilibrium**. At equilibrium, the **quantity demanded** by buyers exactly equals the **quantity supplied** by sellers, so there is no leftover stock and no unmet desire to buy. Graphically, it's simply the point where the downward-sloping demand curve crosses the upward-sloping supply curve.

To see why this point is special, consider what happens when the price is *too high*. Suppose tomatoes are priced at $5 a kilo, but at that price farmers want to sell 1,000 kilos while shoppers only want 600. The result is a **surplus** of 400 kilos, unsold tomatoes piling up and starting to rot. Sellers respond the only sensible way: they cut prices to move the excess. As the price falls, quantity demanded rises and quantity supplied shrinks, until the gap closes.

The mirror image happens when the price is *too low*. At $1 a kilo, shoppers might want 1,200 kilos while farmers only offer 500, leaving a **shortage** of 700. Frustrated buyers compete for the scarce supply, and sellers notice they can charge more. The price gets bid upward, choking off some demand and encouraging more supply until balance returns. This automatic push toward equilibrium, surpluses driving prices down and shortages driving them up, is what economists mean when they say a market **clears**.

What makes this so powerful is that no one is in charge. There's no committee deciding the right price for tomatoes; it arises spontaneously from thousands of independent decisions. The price acts as a signal that coordinates strangers, telling producers how much to grow and consumers how much to buy. Adam Smith famously called this the "invisible hand."

Equilibrium isn't frozen, though, it moves whenever a curve **shifts**:

- **Demand rises** (curve shifts right), supply unchanged: price *and* quantity both rise. A heatwave boosting demand for ice cream raises its price and the amount sold.
- **Demand falls**: price and quantity both drop.
- **Supply rises** (curve shifts right), demand unchanged: price *falls* but quantity *rises*. A bumper harvest makes tomatoes cheaper and more plentiful.
- **Supply falls**: price rises and quantity falls.

When *both* curves shift at once, the direction of one variable becomes ambiguous and depends on which shift is larger, a reminder to analyze each curve carefully. This framework, two simple curves meeting and re-meeting, explains an astonishing range of real-world price movements.
