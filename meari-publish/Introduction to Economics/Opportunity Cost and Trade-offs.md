## Quick Summary
Opportunity cost is the value of the next best alternative you give up when you make a choice; it is the *true* cost of any decision, going beyond the money you spend. If a student spends a Saturday working a $100 shift, the opportunity cost is the studying, leisure, or other job they sacrificed. Because resources are scarce, every choice is a trade-off, and opportunity cost makes that trade-off concrete.

When economists ask "What does this cost?" they mean something deeper than the price tag. The real cost of any choice is **opportunity cost**: the value of the *next best alternative you give up* in order to do what you chose. Suppose you have a free evening and you go to a concert that costs $50. The true cost is not just the $50—it also includes whatever you would otherwise have done with that evening and that money, perhaps a quiet night studying that would have raised your grade, or an extra shift earning $80. Because resources like time, money, and attention are scarce (see [[Scarcity and the Economic Problem]]), choosing one thing always means forgoing another. There is no such thing as a free lunch.

Opportunity cost explains decisions that look puzzling on the surface. A talented surgeon earning $400 an hour might still pay someone $30 an hour to mow the lawn, even if the surgeon could mow it faster. The reason is that an hour spent mowing is an hour *not* spent in surgery—so the opportunity cost of the surgeon's own labour is enormous. This logic of comparing what you give up is the seed of [[Comparative Advantage and International Trade]], where whole countries gain by specialising in what they sacrifice least to produce.

To see trade-offs for an entire economy, economists draw the **production possibilities frontier (PPF)**. Imagine a country that can make only two things, say computers and wheat, using its fixed supply of workers and machines. The PPF is a curve showing every maximum combination it could produce:

- Points **on** the curve are **efficient**—all resources are fully and well used.
- Points **inside** the curve are **inefficient** or reflect idle resources (unemployment, unused factories).
- Points **outside** the curve are currently **unattainable** without more resources or better technology.

Moving along the frontier captures opportunity cost directly: to make 100 more computers you must shift workers and materials away from wheat, so you grow less wheat. The amount of wheat sacrificed per extra computer *is* the opportunity cost, and it equals the slope of the curve. The PPF usually **bows outward** rather than being a straight line, because resources are not equally good at everything. The first workers moved from wheat to computers might be those least suited to farming, so little wheat is lost; but as you keep shifting, you start pulling away skilled farmers, and each additional computer costs more and more wheat. This is the **law of increasing opportunity cost**.

A final, crucial distinction is the **sunk cost**. A sunk cost is money, time, or effort already spent that *cannot be recovered* no matter what you do next. Rational decision-making ignores sunk costs entirely, because they are the same under every future option. If you have paid $40 for a movie ticket and, twenty minutes in, the film is dreadful, the $40 is gone whether you stay or leave. The only relevant question is forward-looking: is the next ninety minutes better spent watching or doing something else? People often fall into the **sunk-cost fallacy**—"I've already invested so much, I can't quit now"—and throw good resources after bad. Sound economic thinking, which you will sharpen in [[Marginal Thinking and Incentives]], always compares the *additional* future benefits and costs of a choice, never the unrecoverable past.
