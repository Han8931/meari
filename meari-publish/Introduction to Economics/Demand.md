## Quick Summary
Demand describes how much of a good buyers are willing and able to purchase at each price, and the law of demand says that, holding everything else constant, a higher price reduces quantity demanded while a lower price increases it. This produces a downward-sloping demand curve. A price change causes a movement along the curve, changing quantity demanded; it does not shift the curve.

When economists talk about **demand**, they don't just mean "wanting" something. Demand refers to how much of a good buyers are both *willing and able* to purchase at each possible price. You might want a sports car, but if you can't pay for it, that desire isn't part of market demand. Demand is always measured at specific prices, which is why it's best thought of as a whole schedule rather than a single number.

The cornerstone of this idea is the **law of demand**: all else being equal, when the price of a good rises, the quantity people want to buy falls, and when the price falls, the quantity rises. This inverse relationship is remarkably reliable. Imagine a coffee shop selling lattes at $5 and selling 100 a day. Drop the price to $4 and they might sell 140; raise it to $6 and sales could fall to 70. Plotting price against quantity gives the familiar downward-sloping **demand curve**.

A crucial distinction trips up many beginners: the difference between a **movement along** the curve and a **shift** of the curve. When only the good's *own price* changes, we slide along the existing curve, this is a change in *quantity demanded*. The latte example above is a movement along the curve. The whole curve only moves when something *other* than price changes, which we call a change in *demand* itself.

What can shift the entire curve? Economists group these **determinants of demand** into a handful of categories:

- **Income** — for *normal goods*, more income shifts demand right; for *inferior goods* (like instant noodles), more income shifts it left.
- **Tastes and preferences** — a viral health trend can send demand for oat milk soaring.
- **Prices of related goods** — if the price of tea (a *substitute*) jumps, coffee demand rises; if the price of pastries (a *complement*) falls, coffee demand may rise too.
- **Expectations** — if buyers expect prices to climb next month, they buy more now.
- **Number of buyers** — a growing population or a new market expands demand.

Keeping this straight matters because it changes your predictions. If you see lattes selling faster, you must ask *why*. A sale (lower price) is a movement down a fixed curve, but a cold winter boosting hot-drink cravings shifts the curve rightward, meaning more lattes sell *at every price*. Demand never works alone, though: to understand actual prices we pair it with [[Supply]] and study how the two meet at [[Market Equilibrium]].
