## Quick Summary
Scarcity is the central economic problem: human wants are effectively unlimited, but the resources to satisfy them—land, labour, capital, time—are finite. Because we cannot have everything, every society must make choices, and those choices boil down to three basic questions. First, *what* goods and services to produce (more hospitals or more highways?).

Imagine you have $20 and you are standing in a shop full of things you would happily own. You want the headphones, the book, the lunch, and the phone case—but $20 buys only a fraction of them. This everyday squeeze is the heart of economics. **Scarcity** is the condition that arises because human wants are essentially unlimited, while the resources available to satisfy those wants are limited. It is not the same as poverty or shortage; even a billionaire faces scarcity, because time and attention remain finite. Scarcity is universal, and it is the reason economics exists at all: if everything were free and infinite, there would be nothing to decide.

The resources that scarcity constrains are often grouped as the **factors of production**: land (natural resources), labour (human effort and skill), and capital (tools, machines, and buildings), sometimes with entrepreneurship added as a fourth. Because these inputs are limited, no society can produce everything its members might want. Every choice to use resources one way is simultaneously a choice not to use them another way—an idea explored more fully in [[Opportunity Cost and Trade-offs]].

Because resources are scarce, every society—from a hunter-gatherer band to a modern industrial nation—must answer three basic questions:

- **What to produce?** Should a country devote its steel and engineers to building tanks, bridges, or wind turbines? With limited inputs, more of one means less of another.
- **How to produce it?** A farm can be worked by many labourers with hand tools or by a few people with expensive machinery. The "right" method depends on what is scarce and what is cheap.
- **For whom to produce?** Once goods exist, who gets them? Distribution can follow ability to pay, government rationing, need, or political power.

Different systems answer these questions with different tools. In a **market economy**, prices act as signals: when something is scarce and wanted, its price rises, nudging producers to make more and consumers to economize. In a **command economy**, central planners decide directly. In practice almost every real economy is a **mixed economy**, leaning on markets for most goods while governments handle things like defence, roads, and schools.

Economists study these decisions at two scales. **Microeconomics** looks at the small picture—individual households deciding how to spend, firms deciding what to charge, and single markets like the market for coffee or rental apartments. **Macroeconomics** looks at the big picture—the whole economy at once, tracking aggregates such as total output (see [[Measuring the Economy with GDP]]), the general price level, and the share of people without jobs (see [[Inflation and Unemployment]]). The two levels are deeply connected: a million micro-decisions about hiring and spending add up to the macro outcomes we read about in the news.

Keep scarcity in mind as you study everything that follows. Demand, supply, costs, trade, taxes, and central-bank policy are all, at bottom, society's evolving answers to the same stubborn fact: we cannot have it all, so we must choose.
