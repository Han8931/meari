## Quick Summary
When a headline reports "GDP grew 0.4% in the quarter," it means the total value of goods and services the economy produced expanded modestly over three months; growth figures are the heartbeat of the **business cycle** of expansion and contraction. The popular rule of thumb defines a **recession** as two consecutive quarters of negative GDP growth, but in practice officials weigh jobs, income, and spending too, so a real recession means a broad, lasting decline — not a single soft quarter, which is just a slowdown.

Economies do not move in a straight line; they breathe in and out through what economists call the **business cycle** — alternating periods of **expansion** (growth) and **contraction** (decline), with peaks and troughs in between. The news tracks this rhythm mainly through one number: **GDP growth**. A headline like "**Economy grew 0.4% in the third quarter**" means the total value of everything the country produced — see [[Measuring the Economy with GDP]] — rose 0.4% over three months. Whether that is good depends on context: 0.4% quarterly is healthy for a rich country, alarming for a fast-developing one.

The phrase everyone reaches for is **recession**, and the popular **rule of thumb** is "**two consecutive quarters of negative growth**." It is a handy shortcut, but treat it as a rough guide, not gospel. Official bodies (like the NBER in the US) declare recessions by looking at a *bundle* of evidence — employment, real income, industrial production, spending — and asking whether the decline is **deep, broad, and lasting**. So a single weak quarter is usually just a **slowdown**; a true recession is a sustained, economy-wide retreat. Distinguishing the two keeps you from panicking at every soft data point.

Because GDP is reported with a lag, savvy readers watch **leading indicators** — signals that tend to turn *before* the economy does:

- **The yield curve** — normally, long-term government bonds pay higher interest than short-term ones, because lending for longer carries more risk. When this flips and short-term yields exceed long-term ones, the curve has **inverted**. An inverted yield curve has preceded nearly every modern US recession, because it shows investors expect the central bank to *cut* rates later to rescue a weakening economy.
- **Purchasing managers' indexes (PMIs)** — surveys of businesses; a reading below 50 signals contraction.
- **Consumer confidence and new orders** — early hints that households and firms are pulling back.

Unemployment, by contrast, is a **lagging indicator**: it usually rises *after* a downturn has already begun, because firms cut jobs only once demand has clearly fallen. This is why a broadcast can simultaneously report "yield curve inverts, recession feared" and "unemployment at record lows" without contradiction — the warning sign (the curve) leads, and the job losses, if they come, follow months later. Headlines like "**Unemployment ticks up to 4.2%**" or "**Jobless claims rise**" are confirming the story, not predicting it, and connect directly to the tension explored in [[Inflation and Unemployment]].

So when recession talk dominates the news, resist the urge to fixate on one statistic. Read the signals as a chorus: Is GDP growth slowing across several quarters or just one? Has the yield curve inverted, and for how long? Are leading indicators rolling over while unemployment is still low — the classic late-cycle pattern? A recession is a *process*, and the most useful thing a reader can do is recognize where in the business cycle the economy seems to be, rather than chase the scariest number of the day.
