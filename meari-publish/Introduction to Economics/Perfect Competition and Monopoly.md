## Quick Summary
Market structures form a spectrum defined by how much control a single seller has over price. At one extreme is perfect competition: many small firms selling identical products, where each is a price-taker that must accept the going market price (a wheat farmer cannot charge more than $7 a bushel). At the other extreme is monopoly: a single seller of a product with no close substitutes, a price-maker that can raise price by selling less.

Not all markets behave the same way. Economists arrange them along a **spectrum of market structures** defined by one central question: how much power does a single seller have over the price it charges? At one end sits **perfect competition**, where no seller has any pricing power at all. At the other sits **monopoly**, where one seller controls the entire market. Most real industries fall somewhere in between, but understanding the two extremes first makes everything in the middle easier to grasp.

A **perfectly competitive** market has many small firms selling an identical product, with easy entry and exit and buyers who know the going price. The classic example is a commodity like wheat. A single farmer growing wheat is a **price-taker**: if the market price is $7 a bushel, that farmer must accept $7. Charge $7.50 and buyers simply purchase identical wheat from someone else; charge less and you needlessly throw away revenue. Because each firm is tiny relative to the whole market, none can budge the price on its own. Competitive markets tend to push prices down toward the cost of production and squeeze long-run economic profit toward zero.

A **monopoly** is the opposite: a single seller of a product with no close substitutes, protected by barriers that keep rivals out, such as patents, control of a key resource, or high startup costs. A monopolist is a **price-maker**. But its power is not unlimited, because demand still slopes downward, customers buy less as the price rises. To sell more units the monopolist must lower the price, so it faces a genuine trade-off between charging more per unit and selling a larger quantity.

Here lies the social cost of monopoly. To maximize profit, a monopolist deliberately **restricts output** below the level a competitive market would reach and charges a **higher price**. By keeping the product scarcer, it props up the price and pockets extra profit. The problem is that some customers would happily have bought the good at a price above what it costs to make, yet below the monopoly price. Those mutually beneficial trades never happen. The value lost from these forgone transactions is called **deadweight loss**, units that buyers valued more highly than their production cost but that simply were never produced. This is why monopoly is generally considered inefficient even when it is highly profitable.

Pure monopoly and perfect competition are rare in their textbook forms; most industries live between them. **Oligopoly** describes a market dominated by a few large, interdependent firms, where each one's pricing and output decisions depend on what rivals are expected to do. Think of commercial airlines, smartphone makers, or soft-drink giants, where a handful of companies watch one another closely and a price cut by one often triggers a response from the others. **Monopolistic competition** describes many firms selling **differentiated** products, restaurants, coffee shops, clothing brands. Each enjoys a little pricing power because its product is distinctive, but free entry by competitors keeps that power, and long-run profit, modest. Together these structures map the realistic terrain between the two extremes.
