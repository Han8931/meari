## Quick Summary
A market failure occurs when freely operating markets fail to allocate resources efficiently, so the outcome could be improved for society. A common cause is externalities, costs or benefits that fall on third parties who are not part of a transaction. With negative externalities like pollution, the producer ignores the harm to others, so the good is overproduced; with positive externalities like vaccination or education, the buyer ignores the benefit to others, so the good is underproduced.

In a well-functioning competitive market, prices guide resources to where they are valued most, and the outcome is usually efficient. But markets do not always get it right. A **market failure** happens when a free market, left to itself, allocates resources in a way that is inefficient, meaning society could be made better off by doing things differently. Recognizing where markets fail is essential, because it tells us when there may be a genuine case for stepping in, rather than trusting the [[Market Equilibrium]] that supply and demand produce on their own.

One of the most important causes is the **externality**, a cost or benefit that spills over onto a third party who had no say in the transaction. Externalities come in two flavors. A **negative externality** imposes costs on others: a factory that dumps smoke into the air lowers its own costs but forces nearby residents to bear dirty air, asthma, and cleaning bills. Because the factory ignores those costs, it produces **too much** of the good relative to what is best for society. A **positive externality** confers benefits on others: when you get a flu vaccine, you protect not only yourself but also the people around you, and when someone earns a degree, society gains a more skilled citizen. Because buyers ignore these spillover benefits, such goods tend to be **underproduced**.

A second major source of failure is the **public good**. Public goods have two special features: they are **non-excludable** (you cannot easily stop people from enjoying them) and **non-rival** (one person's use does not diminish another's). Think of national defense, a lighthouse, or street lighting. Once the army defends the country or the lamp is lit, everyone benefits whether they paid or not.

This leads straight to the **free-rider problem**. If I can enjoy the fireworks display, the clean air, or the national defense without paying, why would I voluntarily chip in? When everyone reasons this way, too little money is collected and the good is undersupplied or never provided at all. That is why private markets rarely build their own armies or fund basic research, and why governments often step in to provide public goods directly, funded through taxes.

Fortunately, market failures can often be eased with the right policy, each aimed at making private incentives match the true social value. **Taxes** on harmful activities (a tax on each ton of carbon emitted) force polluters to feel the cost they impose, nudging output down toward the efficient level. **Subsidies** for beneficial activities (cheap vaccines, education grants) encourage more of what we want. **Regulation** can simply set limits, such as emissions standards or mandatory schooling. And clearly defined **property rights** can let affected parties bargain a solution themselves, as when a fishing community granted ownership of a lake has every reason to protect it from overuse. None of these fixes is free or perfect, so the art of policy is choosing the remedy whose benefits most outweigh its costs.
