## Quick Summary
Price elasticity of demand measures how responsive quantity demanded is to a price change, calculated as the percentage change in quantity divided by the percentage change in price. Demand is elastic when this exceeds one (quantity responds strongly, as with luxuries or goods with many substitutes) and inelastic when it is below one (quantity barely responds, as with necessities like insulin).

The law of demand tells us that quantity falls when price rises, but it doesn't tell us *by how much*. Sometimes a small price hike sends buyers fleeing; other times they barely notice. **Elasticity** is the tool economists use to measure this responsiveness. The most common version, **price elasticity of demand**, is defined as the percentage change in quantity demanded divided by the percentage change in price. If a 10% price increase cuts sales by 20%, the elasticity is 2.

We sort goods into two camps based on this number. Demand is **elastic** when the figure is greater than 1, meaning quantity responds strongly to price, and **inelastic** when it's less than 1, meaning quantity hardly budges. A movie subscription is elastic: raise the price and many cancel. Insulin is highly inelastic: a diabetic needs it regardless of price.

What makes a good elastic or inelastic? The key **determinants** are:

- **Availability of substitutes** — more alternatives (one brand of cola among many) means more elastic demand.
- **Necessity versus luxury** — necessities like bread are inelastic; luxuries like cruises are elastic.
- **Share of income** — a $1 rise in a $50 item matters more than on a pack of gum, so big-ticket items tend to be more elastic.
- **Time horizon** — demand grows more elastic over time as people find alternatives; a petrol price spike barely changes driving this week but reshapes car-buying over years.

Elasticity has a powerful practical payoff through its link to **total revenue** (price times quantity). When demand is *elastic*, cutting the price actually *raises* revenue, because the jump in quantity sold more than offsets the lower price per unit. When demand is *inelastic*, the opposite holds: raising the price raises revenue, since buyers stick around despite paying more. This is exactly why a struggling theatre might slash ticket prices to fill seats, while a toll authority can hike fares without losing many drivers.

Two related measures round out the picture. **Income elasticity of demand** captures how quantity responds to changes in income; it's positive for *normal goods* and negative for *inferior goods*, distinguishing the two cleanly. **Cross-price elasticity** measures how the demand for one good responds to the price of another, positive for *substitutes* (butter and margarine) and negative for *complements* (printers and ink). Finally, **price elasticity of supply** measures how responsive producers are to price, and it hinges mainly on time and spare capacity: factories with idle machines can ramp up quickly (elastic supply), while farmland or beachfront property is fixed in the short run (inelastic supply). Together these measures let us go beyond the direction of change at [[Market Equilibrium]] and quantify its magnitude.
